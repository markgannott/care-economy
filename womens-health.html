Executive Summary

Three gaps. One thesis.

The women's health market is underserved not because demand is small — it's 54 million patients with $26.6B in documented employer burden — but because capital, research, and infrastructure have systematically underinvested in it. That is changing. The science on menopause and cognitive health is arriving all at once. The digital health correction has left real clinical infrastructure at scrap prices. Employers are adopting meno benefits at 4× the rate of two years ago.

Part I
The Scale
The Market

54 million patients. Still called a niche.

The US women's health economy is anchored by a single demographic cohort that the healthcare system has chronically underserved: 54 million women in perimenopause or menopause. That is not a niche — it is larger than the entire US diabetic population. The burden is documented, the patients are employed, and the infrastructure to serve them is fragmented and early.

Structural Point

The 54M figure is a floor, not a ceiling. It counts only women currently in the peri/menopausal window. The downstream population — post-menopausal women with elevated dementia, cardiovascular, and bone health risk — is substantially larger, and the cost burden compounds for decades after the acute clinical window.

Employer Burden

$26.6 billion that doesn't show up on a benefits line.

The Faubion et al. (2023) analysis of menopause-related employer costs is the only peer-reviewed, employer-level burden estimate available in the literature. It captures absenteeism, presenteeism, and healthcare utilization — and it reaches $26.6B annually in the US alone. This is the number that gets a chief people officer's attention.

Payer Implication

At a 47% healthcare cost differential for menopausal vs. non-menopausal employees (Milliman/Gennev), early intervention that closes even half that gap represents a documented, modelable PMPY reduction — the kind of number that converts a chief medical officer from interested to contracted.

Healthcare Utilization

47% higher costs. The payer math is obvious.

The Milliman/Gennev analysis established a 47% healthcare cost differential between menopausal and non-menopausal women of comparable age. Published intervention data suggests early HRT and comprehensive menopause care reduces total cost of care by 11–13%. That math — applied to a 54M-person population — is the payer business case.

The Biology
The Cognitive Corridor

Menopause is a neurological inflection point.

The emerging science on menopause and cognitive health has reframed the clinical conversation entirely. Menopause is not only an endocrine event — it is a neurological transition. Early menopause carries a documented 47% increased dementia risk. The research is new enough that no platform has been built around it. The window for a first mover is open now.

The White Space

Nobody owns the menopause → cognitive health corridor. Midi Health owns general menopause telehealth. Maven owns fertility and maternity. The specific nexus — early intervention in menopause to prevent downstream dementia — has no category incumbent. The science is less than two years old. The payer cost base is $781B and growing. That is a first-mover opportunity.

Dementia Economics

$781 billion. Payers have to move.

The US dementia cost base has crossed $781B annually — larger than the entire Medicare prescription drug spend. Two-thirds of that burden falls on women, both as patients and as caregivers. At this scale, dementia prevention is no longer a research priority; it is a payer imperative. The menopause → dementia corridor is the most plausible upstream intervention point.

Research Inflection

Rachel Buckley's 2025 work and the Dobson et al. (Age & Ageing 2024) hazard ratio of 1.47 for early menopause → dementia are driving both scientific attention and payer/policy interest. The Alzheimer's Association has made the sex-difference research a funding priority. This is the research inflection that precedes a payer coverage movement — and it is happening now.

Part III
The Capital Signal
The Correction

$29.1B → $10.1B. Real infrastructure at trough prices.

The 2020–2022 digital health boom deployed $58.5B across ~1,741 deals. Much of that capital went into women's health and menopause platforms at valuations that assumed a DTC consumer market that didn't materialize. The correction has been severe and indiscriminate — creating a multi-year buying window for acquirers who can add payer contracting, employer channel, and clinical credibility to distressed-but-viable platforms.

Market Signal

4% to 18%. Employers are ahead of the market.

The fastest-moving buyer in women's health is not the health plan — it is the employer. Menopause benefit adoption has accelerated from 4% to 18% of large employers in two years. That is not organic drift; it is a deliberate response to documented productivity loss and talent retention pressure in a labor market where women over 40 are a critical workforce cohort.

The Commercial Unlock

At 18% employer adoption, menopause benefits have cleared the proof-of-concept bar — they are no longer a benefits novelty. The remaining 82% of large employers represent an addressable sales motion. A platform that can walk into a benefits conversation with Faubion's $26.6B burden number, a 47% cost differential, and a documented 11–13% TCOC reduction does not need to educate the buyer. The buyer already knows the problem. They need a credible solution.

Part IV
What Gets Built
Venture Categories

Four categories. One portfolio thesis.

The Suncoast women's health portfolio is organized around the full arc of women's health: from reproductive care and maternal monitoring through the menopause transition and into the cognitive health corridor that follows. Each category has a specific commercial mechanism — employer channel, payer contracting, clinical evidence, or platform infrastructure.

Menopause & Functional Medicine
The Clinical Core

DTC-to-enterprise conversion: functional medicine and menopause telehealth platforms with real patients and real retention — restructured to contract with employers and payers. The commercial gap is not product; it is channel.

Maternal Health & Monitoring
The Device Layer

Remote monitoring and diagnostic devices for maternal and fetal health — categories with CMS reimbursement pathways and strong health system partnerships. Clinical evidence is built in; the commercial runway is payer and hospital contracting.

Reproductive Health & Fertility
The Innovation Layer

MedTech and biotech tackling reproductive health — from IVF culture media to contraceptive innovation. This is the deepest technology layer in the portfolio: IP-protected, regulatory-pathwayed, and exit-ready to medtech strategics.

Care Coordination & Family Tech
The Platform Layer

Infrastructure that connects women to the care they manage — for themselves and for their families. NICU-to-home transitions, family operating systems, and the care coordination layer that turns fragmented healthcare into a manageable experience.

Portfolio Thesis

The Suncoast women's health portfolio is designed to cover the full clinical arc — from preconception through maternal care, through the working years of menopause, into the cognitive health corridor that follows.
