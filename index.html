Part I
Scale — the $1.6 trillion hiding in plain sight
Valuation Methodology
Paper I

Why the number is $1.6 trillion — not less.

Three methodologies produce three very different valuations of the same caregiving economy. The differences aren’t rounding errors — they reflect a fundamental choice about what question you’re asking.

Minimum Wage Floor

$384B

Question asked: What if we paid caregivers federal minimum wage?

53B hours × $7.25/hr. Values the work at the floor of the US labor market. Lower bound only — not a valuation.

Shadow Wage / Replacement Cost

~$640B

Question asked: What would it cost to hire a professional caregiver?

53B hours × home health aide rate (~$15–17/hr, BLS 2023). The flaw: prices the work at the replacement worker’s wage, not the caregiver’s opportunity cost.

Displaced Labor — Gannott 2026

$1.62T

Question asked: What income did caregivers actually forgo?

53B hours × sex-adjusted opportunity-cost wages ($27.96/hr women, $34.05/hr men), BLS ATUS + ACS 2022. The displaced labor methodology — same framework national accounts use to price informal production.

Why this matters for investors

A $384B market and a $1.62T market imply completely different TAM calculations, company multiples, and LP conviction. The displaced labor approach is the one that belongs on a fund deck.

The Coordination Economy
Paper II

It’s not just the hours. It’s what the hours do to everything else.

Women perform 354 additional unpaid hours per year. But the economic damage isn’t just the time — it’s what that time does to every other hour. Nominal leisure occupied by secondary monitoring. Administrative fragmentation across broken systems. Chronic sleep disruption. A fragmented hour has lower economic value than an uninterrupted one, and caregivers live in fragmented hours.

Throughput restoration — not linear time recovery

The value mechanism isn’t additive. Restored continuity amplifies the yield of all remaining hours. A caregiver who recovers uninterrupted blocks doesn’t just have more time — their remaining time becomes more productive. The platform building this is building compounding economic value, not a time-tracking app.

Platform Economics
Papers I–III

The infrastructure doesn’t exist yet.

Coordination overhead consumes 20–25% of total caregiver time. The companies building coordination, continuity, and compliance infrastructure for 63M caregivers are building picks and shovels before the category has a name on LP decks.

The platform analogy

Alibaba didn’t just build a marketplace — it built the logistics, payments, and trust infrastructure underneath it. The care economy needs the same layer. Intake, care plan execution, respite routing, compliance documentation, outcomes tracking — all currently manual, all fragmented.
