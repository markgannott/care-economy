Paper I · The Scale Argument

Replacement cost is the wrong model.

Caregiving operates continuously across households — not in discrete scheduled services. Opportunity-cost displacement is the structurally correct frame.

Replacement Cost — Old Model
→Assumes care can be purchased in scheduled blocks
→Ignores continuous household responsibility
→Understates burden — treats care as substitutable
→Values the replacement worker, not the caregiver's economic loss
Resolves to $1.01T (AARP methodology)
Displaced Labor — Correct Model ✓
→Reflects fragmented, continuous nature of caregiving
→Captures income foregone across the full caregiving spell
→Women $27.96/hr · Men $34.05/hr · Blended $30.09/hr
→65% of hours provided by women — same gendered distribution as formal paid care
Resolves to $1.6T in 2026 USD at national scale

Paper II · The Coordination Argument

The visible burden understates the true cost.

Non-working hours are not economically equivalent. Leisure frequently remains cognitively occupied through monitoring, interruption readiness, and coordination load. The $1.6T figure counts hours of care. It does not count what happens to the hours around care.

The Argument

Coordination burden — scheduling, logistics, provider communication — is unpaid labor that does not appear in any caregiving hour count. Adults in households with children under 13 spend 1.80 hours/day of nominal leisure simultaneously providing secondary childcare — $18,374/year in cognitively occupied time that registers as rest.

Paper II · Caregiver Health

Caregivers are not just tired. They are economically compromised.

Sleep Deprivation
Higher rates of short sleep duration among caregivers vs. non-caregivers of comparable age. Sleep is productive infrastructure — loss degrades throughput across work, health, and care simultaneously.
Stress & Burnout
High-burden caregivers face 2.2× higher depression risk. Caregiver distress is measurable, persistent, and systematically underpriced in traditional labor economics.
Reduced Cognitive Continuity
Monitoring, interruption readiness, and planning occupy working memory outside caregiving hours. Cognitive load does not stop when direct care stops.
Displaced Preventative Care
Caregivers defer their own health maintenance — compounding future cost. The caregiver becomes a patient. This feedback loop is economically significant and undercounted.

Paper III · The Value Creation Argument

Continuity is productive infrastructure.

Sleep, recovery, exercise, and uninterrupted cognition are not residual leisure. They are economic inputs — and coordination burden degrades all of them. Coordination reduction creates value not just through time savings, but through throughput restoration.

Sleep
Productive infrastructure. 40% of high-burden caregivers report significant sleep disruption — double the non-caregiver rate. $411B annual economic cost (RAND).
Cognitive Continuity
Uninterrupted cognition drives output quality. Coordination overhead degrades working memory, planning, and decision quality — at work and at home.
Throughput Restoration
The yield of remaining time improves when continuity is restored. One hour recovered is worth more than its wage-rate equivalent once compounding quality effects are included.
